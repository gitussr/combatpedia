What is combat sports?

Combat sports are competitive athletic activities in which contestants directly oppose each other using techniques from martial arts or combat training. They differ from martial arts broadly in that they operate under structured rules designed for fair competition and fighter safety.

From the ancient Olympics of 776 BC — which featured wrestling and pankration — to today's global UFC pay-per-view events, combat sports have evolved into one of the world's most-watched athletic categories.

Three Families of Combat Sport

Striking
Boxing, Muay Thai, Kickboxing, Karate. Emphasis on punches, kicks, elbows, and knees delivered while standing.
Grappling
Wrestling, BJJ, Judo, Sambo. Emphasis on takedowns, control, and submissions on the ground.
Mixed
MMA and Combat Sambo blend striking and grappling, demanding competence across all ranges of combat.

What is MMA?

Mixed Martial Arts (MMA) is a full-contact combat sport allowing a wide variety of fighting techniques from a mixture of martial arts traditions. Contestants may fight standing (striking), in the clinch, or on the ground, making it the most technically demanding of all combat sports.

Origins

Modern MMA traces its roots to Brazil's Vale Tudo ("anything goes") matches of the 1920s–80s, popularized by the Gracie family to prove BJJ's dominance. The UFC's debut in 1993 brought the concept to a global audience, and by the 2000s unified rules transformed it from spectacle to sport.

What is the UFC?

The Ultimate Fighting Championship (UFC) is the world's largest MMA promotion, headquartered in Las Vegas, Nevada. Founded in 1993 as a tournament to determine the most effective martial art, it has evolved into a multi-billion dollar sports enterprise operating in over 170 countries.

Purchased by Dana White and the Fertitta brothers in 2001 for $2 million, the UFC is now valued at over $10 billion. The organization hosts roughly 40–45 events per year, including Pay-Per-View, Fight Night, and The Ultimate Fighter series.

Technique Reference

SUBMISSIONS
ENCYCLOPEDIA

Every major choke and joint lock, organized by type and entry.

A submission is a technique applied to force an opponent to "tap out" — signal defeat — or risk injury (joint damage) or unconsciousness (blood/air choke). Submissions fall into two families: chokes and joint locks.

Chokes

Chokes work by compressing the carotid arteries (blood choke) or trachea (air choke), causing unconsciousness within 3–10 seconds for blood chokes. Blood chokes are far more effective and less painful than air chokes.

Rear Naked Choke
The most finished submission in MMA. Applied from back control, one arm across the throat, the other arm locks behind the head. Pure blood choke — lights out in seconds.
Guillotine Choke
Applied when an opponent shoots for a takedown. The arm wraps under the chin and squeezes up. Can be finished standing or from guard position.
Triangle Choke
Applied with the legs from guard. One arm and the neck are trapped inside a triangle formed by the attacker's legs, cutting off blood flow.
D'Arce Choke
A no-gi variation of the arm triangle. The arm thread under the armpit and across the neck, typically from side control or half guard scrambles.
Anaconda Choke
Similar to the D'Arce but the arm goes under both the neck and arm, pulling the opponent into a forward roll for extra pressure.
Von Flue Choke
A rare and spectacular counter to a guillotine. When an opponent holds a guillotine from guard, the top fighter applies pressure with the shoulder to choke them out.
Joint Locks

Joint Locks

Joint locks hyperextend or rotate joints beyond their natural range of motion. They force a tap via pain and the threat of structural damage to ligaments and tendons.

Armbar
The most iconic submission. The elbow joint is hyperextended across the hip or thigh. Available from mount, guard, side control, and back.
Kimura
Two-on-one shoulder lock with the arm bent behind the back. Named after Masahiko Kimura. Sets up from half guard, north-south, and standing.
Americana
A reverse Kimura — the arm is bent at 90° and rotated toward the head. Most common from mount and side control against a less experienced opponent.
Omoplata
A shoulder lock applied with the legs from guard. The leg traps the arm and the hips rotate to apply pressure. Also serves as a sweep setup.
Heel Hook
The most dangerous leg lock. The heel is trapped and rotated to torque the knee. Inside and outside variations exist — the inside heel hook has ended many careers.
Knee Bar
The knee equivalent of an armbar. The joint is hyperextended. Allowed in advanced BJJ competition and in MMA — hard to defend when entered from leg entanglement.
Toe Hold
Rotates the ankle by gripping the foot. A figure-four grip applies pressure. Often comes as a natural reaction when a heel hook attempt is defended.
Wrist Lock
Rotates or bends the wrist joint. Subtle and surprising — often caught accidentally during other grappling exchanges, especially in BJJ competition.
Grappling Art

BRAZILIAN
JIU-JITSU

The gentle art. Ground fighting mastery built on leverage, not strength.

Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu (BJJ) is a grappling martial art and combat sport focused on ground fighting and submission holds. Its core philosophy: a smaller, weaker person can successfully defend against a larger opponent using leverage, technique, and positional control.

The Gracie Legacy

BJJ was developed in Brazil from Judo techniques taught by Mitsuyo Maeda to Carlos Gracie in 1917. The Gracie family — particularly Hélio Gracie — adapted and refined the art for smaller individuals, emphasizing ground fighting over throws. The family's public challenge matches proved its effectiveness against all other styles.

Guard Variations

Closed Guard
Legs locked around opponent's waist. Defensive and offensive — controls posture, hunts for armbars, triangles, and sweeps.
Open Guard
Legs unlocked but active. Allows more dynamic movement and access to many guard variations.
Butterfly Guard
Feet hooked inside opponent's thighs. Powerful sweeping guard — lifts opponents with the legs to reverse position.
De La Riva
One leg wrapped around the outside of the opponent's thigh. Entry to back takes, leg locks, and the berimbolo series.
X Guard
Both legs form an "X" beneath the standing opponent. Excellent for off-balancing and transitioning to leg attacks.
Deep Half Guard
Deeply underneath the opponent. Creates access to waist lock sweeps and back takes. Hard to submit from here.
Grappling Base

WRESTLING
ENCYCLOPEDIA

The most proven combat sports base. Takedowns, control, and relentless pressure.

Wrestling is the oldest combat sport, present at the ancient Olympics and practiced across every human culture. In modern MMA, a wrestling base is considered among the most valuable — it gives fighters control over where the fight takes place.

Wrestling Styles

Freestyle
International competition style. Leg attacks and throws allowed. Both upper and lower body fair game. Used in Olympic wrestling.
Greco-Roman
No attacks below the waist. Emphasis on upper body throws and clinch domination. Produces tremendous upper body strength.
Folkstyle
American collegiate and scholastic wrestling. Emphasis on control, riding, and escapes on the mat. Most US MMA wrestlers come from this background.

The Sprawl — Essential MMA Defense

The sprawl is wrestling's most important defensive technique in MMA. When an opponent shoots for a takedown, the defender throws their hips backward and down, driving the opponent's head into the mat and negating the attempt. Every MMA fighter must develop a reliable sprawl.

Striking Art

MUAY THAI

The Art of Eight Limbs — punches, kicks, elbows, and knees.

Muay Thai is the national sport and martial art of Thailand, practiced for centuries as military combat before becoming a competitive sport. It earns the name "Art of Eight Limbs" for its use of fists (2), elbows (2), knees (2), and shins/feet (2) as weapons.

Eight Weapons

Punches
Jab, cross, hook, uppercut. The setup tools — Muay Thai punching is effective but secondary to kicks and knees.
Kicks
Powerful roundhouse kicks delivered with the shin (not the foot). Low kicks, body kicks, and head kicks — the shin conditioned through bone hardening.
Elbows
Razor-sharp close-range strikes. Cut opponents, create blood stoppages. Horizontal, upward, downward, and spinning variations.
Knees
Devastating in the clinch and from range. Jumping knees (the "Teep" in Thai) are a signature weapon — can stop opponents clean.

The Muay Thai Clinch

Unlike boxing's clinch (broken up by referees), the Muay Thai clinch is an active, offensive position. Control the neck, pull the posture down, and deliver knees to the body and head. Clinch specialists like Diesellek have built entire careers from this range alone.

Sweet Science

BOXING
ENCYCLOPEDIA

The oldest modern combat sport. Footwork, timing, and the four punches.

Boxing is a combat sport in which two fighters throw punches at each other within a roped square ring, wearing padded gloves. It is one of the oldest and most globally recognized sports, with professional boxing operating since the 19th century.

The Four Punches

Jab (1)
Lead hand, straight punch. Fast, low-commitment. Sets range, keeps opponent honest, and sets up power shots.
Cross (2)
Rear hand, straight punch. The primary power punch. Travels across the body with full hip rotation for maximum force.
Hook (3)
Lead or rear hand in a looping arc. Targets the jaw or body. The classic knockout punch — comes from the blind side.
Uppercut (4)
Vertical rising punch targeting the chin. Effective in close quarters. The "check hook" as a counter is a boxing classic.

Olympic Sport

JUDO
ENCYCLOPEDIA

The gentle way — throws, pins, and Olympic glory.

Judo was created in 1882 by Jigoro Kano as a martial art and educational system derived from jujutsu. It became an Olympic sport in 1964. The word means "gentle way" — not because it is soft, but because it uses an opponent's force against them rather than meeting force with force.

Judo's Influence on MMA

Judokas like Ronda Rousey and Fedor Emelianenko brought throwing and hip-throw-based takedowns into MMA, demonstrating that world-class throws work even against resisting fighters. The over-the-shoulder throw (seoi nage) and hip throw (o-goshi) are now part of the MMA toolbox.
